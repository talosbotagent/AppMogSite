# Branding Research: Your App Building Studio

## The Landscape You're Entering

The audience you're targeting — vibe coders, indie hackers, and people curious about how apps get built — is booming. Vibe coding was named Collins Dictionary's Word of the Year for 2025, and the community around it has exploded. Platforms like Vercel and Netlify have seen massive user base growth driven almost entirely by this new wave of builders. Your timing is excellent.

The "build in public" movement is the cultural backbone here. Figures like Pieter Levels (Nomad List, RemoteOK) and Marc Lou (ShipFast) have proven that transparency, personality, and showing your work attracts loyal audiences. Marc Lou went from zero income to $50K+/month by shipping fast and documenting everything — his personal brand *was* the product marketing.

The key insight: in this space, **the builder IS the brand**. Your audience doesn't want corporate polish. They want to see the mess, the decisions, the wins, and the failures.

---

## Brand Positioning Strategy

### What Works in This Space

**Personal-first, studio-second.** The most successful indie studios lead with the founder's personality. Think of it less like "Apple" and more like a YouTube channel that happens to ship apps. Your name should feel like a person's project, not a company's product.

**"Building in public" as core identity.** Your audience wants the behind-the-scenes: the Claude Code sessions, the App Store rejections, the moment a feature finally clicks. This isn't just content strategy — it's your brand DNA.

**Anti-corporate energy.** The vibe coder audience is specifically drawn to the idea that one person with AI tools can compete with entire teams. Lean into that scrappiness. No stock photos, no mission statements, no "we leverage synergies."

### What Doesn't Work

- Overly polished "agency" branding (screams expense, not relatability)
- Generic tech names (AppForge, CodeVault, PixelCraft — forgettable)
- Anything that sounds like you're selling *to* developers rather than being one of them
- Trying to look bigger than you are — the audience respects solo builders

## Name Concepts

Here are several directions, each with a different flavour. I've tried to hit your criteria: new, exciting, informal, relatable.

### Direction 1: The Workshop / Garage Vibe
Names that evoke a maker's space — where things get built, tinkered with, shipped.

| Name | Why It Works | Vibe |
|------|-------------|------|
| **Tinker Labs** | Playful, hands-on, experimental. Says "we try stuff" | Curious, inventive |
| **ShipIt Studio** | Direct, action-oriented. Nods to indie hacker culture | Fast, no-nonsense |
| **Prompt & Ship** | Acknowledges the AI/vibe coding method openly | Modern, honest |
| **The App Shed** | British, informal, like a garden shed where cool stuff gets built | Humble, authentic |

### Direction 2: The Personality-Led Brand
Where YOU are the brand, and the studio name is secondary.

| Approach | Example | Why It Works |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| **[Name] Ships** | "Tony Ships" | Dead simple. Your audience follows *you*. The name IS the content promise |
| **[Name] Builds** | "Tony Builds" | Same energy, emphasises the craft |
| **Built by [Name]** | "Built by Tony" | Credits you on every app, like a maker's mark |

This is the Marc Lou / Pieter Levels playbook. It's proven. Your apps become episodes in an ongoing series that people follow.

### Direction 3: The Attitude / Energy Name
Names with personality that feel like a manifesto.

| Name | Why It Works | Vibe |
|------|-------------|------|
| **Ship or Die** | Provocative, memorable, captures the indie hacker ethos | Bold, punk |

### Direction 4: The Concept Name
More abstract, but distinctive and brandable.

| Name | Why It Works | Vibe |
|------|-------------|------|
| **Hotfix** | Dev term for an urgent patch. Implies speed and action | Urgent, capable |
| **Sidequest** | Gaming term. Each app is a sidequest. Implies adventure and variety | Fun, exploratory |

## Recommended Direction

Given your situation — building apps like Photo Blitz and Thronglet, wanting to attract vibe coders who are curious about the process — I'd strongly recommend **Direction 2 (personality-led) combined with elements of Direction 3 (attitude)**.

Here's why:

1. **You already have the content.** You're building real apps with real stories. Photo Blitz's journey from concept to App Store, Thronglet's Black Mirror-inspired evolution — these are compelling narratives.

2. **Your audience follows people, not logos.** In the indie/vibe coder space, personal brands dramatically outperform studio brands for engagement.

3. **It's the lowest-friction path.** No need to agonise over the perfect studio name. Start with your name + a verb, and let the brand evolve organically.

4. **It scales both ways.** If you stay solo, great. If you grow, you can always introduce a studio name later. But trying to introduce a *person* into an established *studio* brand is much harder.

### The Recommended Stack

**Primary brand:** A personality-led name (e.g., "Tony Ships" or "Tony Builds" or your own variation)

**Tagline options:**
- "One dev. Real apps. No fluff."
- "Building apps in public, one vibe at a time"
- "Shipping from the shed" (if you lean into the British angle)
- "Ideas in, apps out"

**Secondary/studio name (optional, for App Store credits):** Something from Direction 1 or 4 that you like — used as the developer name on the App Store while your personal brand handles the content.

## Visual Identity Guidance

### Colour Palette
Avoid: corporate blues, startup gradients, generic tech purple.

Go for something with energy and personality:

- **Electric/neon accents** (lime green, hot pink, electric blue) on dark backgrounds — this is the aesthetic of the dev tool / indie hacker world right now
- **Terminal green on dark** — developer culture coded, instantly recognisable
- **Bold, high-contrast** — stands out in Twitter/X feeds and YouTube thumbnails

### Typography
- **Monospace for headings** (JetBrains Mono, Fira Code, IBM Plex Mono) — signals "I'm a builder"
- **Clean sans-serif for body** (Inter, DM Sans) — readable, modern
- Avoid serif fonts entirely — too formal for this audience

### Logo Direction
- A simple wordmark or lettermark is ideal
- Consider incorporating a cursor/caret, terminal prompt, or shipping box icon
- Should work as a small avatar (Twitter, GitHub, App Store)
- Meme-ability matters — if your logo can be turned into a sticker or emoji, you're winning

### Content Style
- Screenshots of actual code and builds (not mockups)
- Screen recordings of Claude Code sessions
- Casual, phone-shot videos over produced content
- Memes and dev humour mixed with genuine updates
- Revenue/download transparency when comfortable

## Content Channels (Priority Order)

1. **X/Twitter** — The home base of indie hackers and vibe coders. Short updates, screenshots, milestones. Use #buildinpublic.
2. **YouTube** — Longer "build with me" content, app walkthroughs, decision-making processes. This is where Marc Lou's brand really took off.
3. **A simple blog or Substack** — For deeper dives, post-mortems, and technical breakdowns.
4. **GitHub** — If you open-source any tools or share code snippets, this builds credibility.
5. **Product Hunt** — For each app launch. Great for initial visibility spikes.

## Action Items

1. **Pick a name direction** — personality-led is the strong recommendation, but go with what feels right
2. **Secure handles** — check X, YouTube, GitHub, and domain availability immediately
3. **Set up a minimal landing page** — even a single-page site listing your apps and linking to your content
4. **Start posting build updates** — even before the "brand" feels ready. Consistency beats perfection in this space
5. **Create a simple visual kit** — avatar, banner, colour scheme. Keep it tight and reusable
6. **Launch Photo Blitz with a build-in-public narrative** — document the final push to the App Store. Your audience loves launch stories
